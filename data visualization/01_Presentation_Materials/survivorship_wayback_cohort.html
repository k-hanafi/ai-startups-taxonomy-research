Survivorship-Bias Recovery

Recovering the Companies
the Live Crawl Never Saw

The live pipeline can only classify companies whose sites Tavily can still read today, which silently biases the dataset toward survivors. This death-anchored Wayback probe recovers a pre-death snapshot from the Internet Archive for the 22,002 companies Tavily could not extract, so they can run through the unchanged classifier. For each company it takes the full capture history, treats the most recent capture as the site's death, and picks the capture closest to 182 days (~6 months) before death — staying out of the parked / dead final-page tail.

01. Recoverability

How Much of the Lost Cohort Is Recoverable

14,963 of 18,013 probed companies (83.1%) yield a clean pre-death snapshot. Only 245 were never archived; the 2,252 errors are transient network / CDX throttles that a re-run recovers.

03. Temporal

When the Cohort Was Last Seen, and What Evidence Era We Recovered

The death anchor (most recent Wayback capture) clusters heavily in 2025–2026: many of these companies were still being archived recently, so they are unextractable rather than long-dead. The chosen snapshot necessarily lags it by ~6 months.

89.2% of recovered snapshots are GenAI-era. 13,342 postdate GPT-4's launch versus 1,621 before it. The pre-death evidence we feed the classifier mostly reflects the same GenAI era the live-web survivors were seen in — keeping the survivorship comparison fair rather than skewing the dead cohort toward older, pre-AI messaging.

04. Composition

Who Is in the Lost Cohort

The not-found cohort skews to recent founders, and a striking share were still flagged live at crawl time — a reminder that “Tavily could not extract” is an extraction failure, not a death certificate.

“Not found” does not mean “dead”. 10,511 of these companies (58.4%) are still flagged live, yet Tavily could not extract them — typically parked, JS-only, or bot-blocked pages. That is precisely the evidence the Wayback recovery restores.
